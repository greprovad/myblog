Sorry for the Delayed Response

November 02, 2018    Churn CVM Tweet Big Data

Last year I posted a blog about one of the interviewed I had and I shared the blog link on Twitter. A friend of mine commented on it and raised a good remark, but I was reluctant to reply since his account name was unfamiliar to me. Now he has changed the account name, I can unmask the unknown behind this great comment. It’s Lionel Andjaki. I am sorry for the delayed response.


Very interesting but you think about process mining. Ability to find not only the wrong node from historical data but the wrong path

— Lionel Andjaki (@landjaki) September 24, 2017

2/ combine historical data, even and process too. This resolve the what 's wrong question. Big data + process that produce them

— Lionel Andjaki (@landjaki) September 24, 2017

Here my reply:


Big data is lazy without efficient assumptions, i.e. (you cannot blindly use it to tackle your survey). You first need to identify the problem. Churn is more generic; thus, we should explore historical data to learn from user behavioral patterns. That's the reason why I mentioned products as a variable of interest. The correlation between product and time may yield actionable patterns to classify with respect to the period they like carrying out operations in the network. Since correlation does not imply causation, I proposed an individual-based approach that would vertically treat each customer case to some extent. The binary factor (happy and unhappy) is to uncover the rationale for the churn avalanche. This basically includes the notion of satisfaction. Having said, we can, at this level, investigate from all sources, e.g. (1) daily reports to verify whether some particular days the network coverage issue has occurred, (2) NOC (network operations center) reports about the quality of BTS (Base Transceiver Station) for a specific region, (3) sales reports to measure products’ penetration by region, etc..


From this analysis, we'll get a reliable dataset of users to submit to the campaign to uplift their activities and to predict the advent of eventual churns.


Users who've tended to churn would see opportunities to activate alluring plans that fit their operational times. They would be tempted to stay and get the advantage of these plans. From the CVM reports, we would conclude whether the campaign was successful or not.


Lionel, what did you mean by "This resolve the what 's wrong question. Big data + process that produce them"?
